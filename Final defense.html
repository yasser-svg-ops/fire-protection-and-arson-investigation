Fire Protection and Arson Investigation is an important subject in criminology that focuses on the prevention of fires, the protection of lives and property, and the investigation of fire incidents. Fire can cause serious damage to homes, buildings, and the environment, which is why proper fire safety knowledge is important for everyone.

Fire protection involves safety equipment and systems designed to prevent or reduce fire damage. Arson investigation focuses on identifying whether a fire was accidental or intentionally set.

Through proper training, fire investigators analyze evidence and burn patterns to determine the cause and origin of fires.

    Lesson 1.4 CHARACTERISTICS OF FLAMMABLE MATERIALS

    Flammable Materials Flammable and Combustible Liquids Flammable and combustible liquids vaporize and form flammable mixtures with air when in open containers, when leaks occur, or when heated.

    To control these potential hazards, several properties of these materials, such as volatility, flashpoint, and flammable range and auto ignition temperatures must be understood. Information on the properties of a specific liquid can be found in that liquid’s safety data sheet (SDS), or other reference material.

    Flammable Aerosols Flammable liquids in pressurized containers may rupture and aerosolize when exposed to heat, creating a highly flammable vapor cloud. As with flammable liquids, these should be stored in a flammable storage cabinet.

    Flammable and Combustible Solids Flammable solids often encountered in the laboratory include alkali metals, magnesium metal, metallic hydrides, some organometallic compounds, and sulfur.

    Many flammable solids react with water and cannot be extinguished with conventional dry chemical or carbon dioxide extinguishers.   • Flammable Liquid PropertiesM

    • Volatility is the tendency or ability of a liquid to vaporize.

    vapor pressure is a measure of a liquid's volatility. A high vapor pressure usually is an indication of a volatile liquid, or one that readily vaporizes. Boiling point is the temperature at which the vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure, such that the pressure of the atmosphere can no longer hold the liquid in a liquid state and bubbles begin to form. In general, a low boiling point indicates a high vapor pressure and, possibly, an increased fire hazard.

    Flashpoint is the minimum temperature at which the vapor concentration near the surface of the liquid is high enough to form an ignitable mixture. Any liquid with a flashpoint less than 100°F is considered to be a flammable liquid. A liquid with a flashpoint between 100°F and 200°F is combustible.

    In general, the relative hazard of a flammable liquid increase as the flashpoint decreases. Flammable range is the proportion of vapor to air mixture that is ignitable and is expressed in terms of percentage of vapor in air by volume. The flammable range is bounded by the Lower Flammable Limit (LFL) and the Upper Flammable Limit (UFL). The LFL is the minimum concentration of flammable liquid vapor in air that will support the propagation of flame, or spread of flame through the entire volume of vapor-air mixture, upon contact with an ignition source. The UFL is the maximum concentration of vapor in air that will support the propagation of flame. It is important to note that vapor-air mixtures below the LFL may burn at the ignition source without propagating away from the point of ignition.

    Auto ignition temperature is the minimum temperature at which a vapor-air mixture will spontaneously ignite, without the necessity of a spark or flame. Vapor density is a measure of a vapor's weight when compared to air. Air is assigned a value of 1. Heavier, or denser, vapors tend to sink to floor level while lighter, less dense vapors tend to rise to ceiling level. This property must be taken into account when working with flammable or combustible liquids outside of fume hoods. Most flammable liquid vapors are heavier than air. These vapors can travel some distance and encounter ignition sources remote from the workstation.

Lesson 3.1 FIRE INVESTIGATION

Fire investigation is by nature the basis for fire prevention program. It is a systematic process of examining the actual scene and gathering information to determine the origin, cause, and development. It involves analyzing burn patterns, fire spread, and location of potential sources, such as electrical outlets.

It is also a complex and tedious methodical approach, combining scientific knowledge and logical sequence.

Note: The Purpose of Primary goal of Fire Investigation is to identify the cause of the fire.

The following are some of the favorite causes listed by firefighters when they are not certain of the actual cause:

a. Faulty wiring

b. Children playing with matches

c. Spontaneous combustion

d. Spark from stove

e. Burning rubbish

f. Careless disposal of smoking material

As far as the investigation is concerned, they must be deemed as:

Cause: that which made the fire start, and

Reason: that which led to the cause of the fire.

Both cause and reason must be established to satisfactorily complete a fire investigation. The “cause” explains the existence of fire, or the WHAT of investigation, while the “reason” establishes the WHY of the fire.

Four General Classes of Fire Causes

1.Natural Cause: Caused naturally, without human intervention or aid, such as by lightning.

2.Accidental Fire: accidental human action causes fire to start.

3.Arson: intentional and criminal human action causes fire to start.

4.Incendiary Fire: fire started by natural action wherein human employment is involved directly or indirectly.

Such as lighting, electrical installation and smoking-related equipment.

Three General Fire Classification of Fire as to Cause

1.Innocent Fire: natural and accidental causes

2.Incendiary fire: intentionally caused, meaning someone deliberately ignited it under circumstances where they knew the fire shouldn’t be started.

3.Unknown Fire: fire of unknown causes.

    Lesson 3.2 – PHOTOGRAPHING THE FIRE SCENE

    It is normally impractical to take physical evidence from a fire scene to a court room so the investigator must rely on the use of photographs to document much of the evidence and to support the observations, opinions and conclusions the investigator wishes to make as to the cause of the fire. As well as supporting the investigator’s conclusions, the photographs should also provide evidence which would counteract arguments of alternative causes of the fire which might be suggested at a later date.

    Photographs are also useful for jogging the investigator’s memory at the time of writing the report or before appearing in court. Also, in extremely dark fire scenes the photographs could provide detail which may not be available using torch light.

    A conclusion made by the investigator which is well supported by photographs can often lead to a guilty plea or, in a civil case, settlement of an issue without trial.

    The photograph provides a pictorial representation which is easily understood and evaluated in the context of the average viewer's experience, as most people are able to interpret what they can see. In a report, the investigator should provide at least one or two photographs wherever possible to support each observation he or she wishes to state.

    The photographs should be numbered and captioned and referred to as "Photograph 1, 2 etc." A plan should also be made showing the view angles of each photograph or alternatively the photographs should be presented in a sequence which will provide some type of continuity to allow the reader to determine the view angle of each one.

    In former times an investigator needed to have considerable photographic skills to produce quality evidence suitable for use in court. Producing photographs at a fire scene which is normally dark, drab and often dangerous was a difficult task as the photographer had to read and adjust the camera settings in torch light and if all went well the investigator found out several days after the inspection what technical aspect of the exercise had been overlooked.

    1. PHOTOGRAPHING THE EXTERIOR

    Where to photograph and what to photograph? The exterior of the building should be photographed a. From all sides or at least two opposite corners to show the overall degree of fire damage and the locations of the various entrances and windows.

    b. The location of the building to neighboring buildings or firefighting access areas should also be made apparent.

    c. As well as any objects which may be material to the circumstances of the fire, such as the location of nearby power poles. The investigator may need to prove at a later date the fire had not spread from such an outside source.

    d. Evidence of tire tracks, footprints and the location of explosion debris should also be photographed to show their relationship with the building and they should be photographed in close detail, as well as from a distance.

    The exterior photographs generally show the nature and material of construction and the locations of the doors, windows, chimneys, flues, electricity fuse box or gas meter. The openings where the fire vented are usually clearly shown in the exterior photographs.

    If the fire started inside a building near an exterior wall or in a roof cavity the area of fire origin can often be determined from studying the heat or burn patterns on the exterior of the window frames, wall or roof. Exterior burn patterns are generally easier to interpret than the equivalent interior burn pattern as they generally provide good color and texture contrast, and are less affected by smoke.

    Panoramic views are valuable in reports as they provide in great detail an overall view of the scene. They can be produced by joining together a sequence of photographs or by using a wide angled lens or a disposable camera equipped with a wide angled lens.

    The investigator should seriously consider carrying one in the car, as a sequence of panoramic photographs of a large fire scene significantly enhances a report.

    Overhead shots of the fire scene should be taken if access can be made through the use of a ladder, cherry picker or a nearby building. The overhead shot provides the viewer with an idea of the plan of the building and can be used as a reference by the viewer to determine the location of objects shown in later photographs. In large warehouse fires where the structure collapses sequentially as a response to the spread

    of the fire, an overhead shot is invaluable for showing the manner in which the structural members have collapsed. If photographs of the actual fire can be obtained from eyewitnesses or media sources then they should be included in the report.

    . PHOTOGRAPHING THE INTERIOR

    Photographs of the interior of a building are generally more difficult for the viewer to interpret than the exterior photographs as the interior surfaces are usually burnt and covered with a layer of soot, and the viewer is generally unfamiliar with such an environment. A good quality flash unit is essential as the photographs need to provide as much detail as possible to allow the viewer to familiarize themselves with the nature of the damage and possibly the identity and purpose of the room before the fire.

    Where to photograph and what to photograph?

    a. All rooms and areas inside the building affected by the fire should be photographed, and the photographs should be sequentially presented in a report from the areas of least damage to the areas of greatest damage, or to the area of fire origin, if this is not the area of greatest damage.

    b. Photographs of the peripheral areas where the fire had not originated should be presented and discussed first in a report so these areas can be eliminated from further discussion.

    c. When photographing each rooms the walls, ceilings and doorways should be shown and, unless a wide angled lens is used, several photographs of each room may be needed.

    d. A prominent object such as a fireplace or a large appliance can be used as a reference point in sequential photographs to allow the viewer to maintain their perspective and their familiarization with the fire scene.

    e. The area of fire origin should be photographed before excavation has commenced and again photographed during excavation, if the process has yielded points of interest. The floor should be cleaned so a photograph can be taken to show the nature of the flooring materials. If the floor has completely combusted and a nearby wall has survived then the original floor level can be marked on the wall through the use of a string and some chalk, and the chalk line photographed.

    f. At areas where an accelerant is suspected the areas of localized damage to the floor as well as the overhead damage should be photographed. The amount of overhead damage should be well documented by photographs as the continued combustion at floor level of combustible materials which, have fallen from above could account for the localized damage at floor level. If debris samples are taken then the sample container should be numbered or labelled and the container photographed at the point of sampling.

    g. The burn patterns at doorways should be photographed to show the spread of the fire between rooms. The burn patterns on the walls in hallways normally show the room from which the fire originated and the direction of spread of the fire.

    h. If appliances are suspected of causing the fire they should be photographed from a distance and in close up in the location where they were found, the remains should then be taken outside to allow closer inspection and so that they can be photographed in sunlight or later in the laboratory.

    3. POINTS OF ENTRY AND SECURITY OF BUILDING

    Fire Brigade personnel will usually give an account of the security of a building as they found it. In many cases their accounts will need to be verified at each point of entry by an inspection of the burn patterns and soot deposits to surfaces around the point of entry, and an examination for tool marks.

  • Where to photograph and what to photograph.>

    • a. If possible the doors should be first photographed in the position in which they were found. If the door was found closed then both sides of the door should be photographed.

    b. The burn patterns and soot deposits around the door and the door frame should be photographed as they normally ascertain the position of the door at the time of the fire.

    c. Any parts of the door or lock which may have been dislodged as a result of some type of forced entry should be photographed at the location where they were found and later reconstructed at their original position. Photographs of the door jamb may also be vital.

    d. The smoke and heat patterns above a window can also indicate the position of the window at the time of the fire.

    f. The window locks should also be recovered and photographed.

    4. CAR FIRES

    If possible the remains of burnt cars should be photographed at the scene and the surrounding area should also be photographed. A fire where the car has burnt in an isolated area can usually be regarded as being deliberately lit. Conversely a fire where the car has burnt in view of independent witnesses can normally be regarded as being accidental in nature. If the car has been transported to a wrecking yard then attempts should be made to examine the original scene as evidence such as petrol trail residues or bonfires which were originally under the car can sometimes be found.

    a.The exterior of the car should ideally be photographed from all sides and then again with the bonnet and doors opened. Cars generally have three compartments being the engine, passenger and boot compartments and all three compartments should be examined and photographed.

    b.The engine and the underside of the bonnet should be photographed and parts such as the fuel line connection, air filter, carburettor, radiator and associated hoses, exhaust manifold, battery, terminals and electrical wiring looms photographed in detail. For comparison, a photograph of the same engine from an unburnt car can be shown next to that of the burnt engine and the major parts of the engine labelled.

    c.A good overall view of the passenger compartment can normally be made by kneeling on the bonnet or the boot and photographing the seats, doors and dashboard. Such a photograph can often show the area of severest fire damage which is generally the area of fire origin as cars have a relatively uniform distribution of naturally available fuel such as plastics.

    d.The window winding mechanisms, window glass and the smoke deposits on the hinges can establish the status of the windows and doors at the time of the fire and should be photographed. Any damage which may indicate the car had been stolen should be photographed.

    LESSON 4: HISTORICAL AND SCIENTIFIC BACKGROUND OF FIRE

    Early humans used fire to warm themselves, cook food, and frighten away predators, enabling them to help their ancestors to travel out of Africa and colonize many regions of the world. Fire thus helped shape human society.

    As early civilizations developed, people discovered more uses of fire. They used fire to provide light, to make tools and weapons, and as a weapon of war. Early religions often involved fire rituals to honor the flames, emphasizing the importance of fire in society. These rituals focused on fire’s power that stemmed from the story of Vesta, the Roman goddess of the hearth.

    In honor, Vesta, the high priest in the Roman religion periodically chose the priestesses called Vestal Virgins, to keep the fire in a community’s hearth burning, symbolizing a fire, especially a central fire in communal life. Before the invention of modern implements starting a fire, especially by several means, usually required time and labor to produce sufficient friction to ignite a fire.

    Today, people mostly focus not on starting fires but on using fire productivity and on preventing or extinguishing unwanted fires. We use fire to cook our food, to heat our homes, and to power plants that produce electricity. At the same time, fire remains a potentially destructive force in people’s lives.

    EARLY FIRE-MAKING TECHNIQUE Early people also learned to make fires by rubbing together pieces of wood until the wood produced a glowing spark that could be used to ignite tinder. Evidence of this has been found in many cultures. One of the most common ways of making fire was the bow drill.     CHEMISTRY OF FIRE: The results from a rapid chemical reaction between a fuel — such as wood, gasoline, and oxygen — and the wind combine to give the oxidation reaction that produces fire. Combustion generates light, heat, flame, and soot.

    How Combustion Occurs Several important factors need to be present for combustion to occur. The first requirements are fuel and oxygen. The fire may range from trees in a forest, gasoline in an automobile. The oxygen is usually from the air in the surrounding air. The next requirement for combustion is an ignition source. The ignition source may be in the form of a spark, a flame, or intense heat from a source of ignition. The ignition source must provide enough energy to start the chemical reaction. Finally, a chemical chain reaction (reaction that continually feeds itself) must occur between the fuel and oxygen for combustion to take place.

    LESSON 2. FIRE CODE TAXES, FEES/CHARGES AND FINES

    The Fire Code of the Philippines (RA 9514) mandates the Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP) to collect taxes, fees, and fines to fund its operations and the modernization of fire services. A portion of these revenues is also allocated to local government units (LGUs) for their local fire stations.

    The Fire Code of the Philippines (Republic Act No. 9514) outlines various penalties for violations, including administrative fines of up to ₱50,000, potential building closures, and in serious cases, criminal penalties like imprisonment. Specifically, fines can be issued for failing to comply with fire safety regulations, and willful non-compliance can lead to imprisonment of up to six years or a fine of up to ₱20,000.

    Fire Code taxes in the Philippines are levied to support fire safety measures and the Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP). They are categorized as Fire Code Realty Tax, Fire Code Construction Tax, and Fire Code Sales Tax.

    Rule 12 of the Revised Implementing Rules and Regulations (RIRR) of Republic Act (RA) 9514, the Fire Code of the Philippines, outlines provisions related to the fire safety inspections, fire safety construction, and the issuance of Fire Safety Inspection Certificate (FSIC). It also covers the assessment and collection of fire code fees and charges.

    Fire Safety Inspection:

    Buildings and structures are inspected to ensure compliance with fire safety standards and the proper installation of fire protection systems.

    A Fire Safety Inspection Certificate (FSIC) is issued after inspection, confirming that the required fire safety construction and protection systems are in place.

    Fire Code Fees:

    Fire Code fees, taxes, and charges are assessed and collected to support the Bureau of Fire Protection (BFP).

    Penalties:

    Violations of the Fire Code can result in penalties, including fines and suspension of business permits.

    Fire Safety Plans:

    Building owners are responsible for submitting fire safety plans for review and approval by the BFP.

    Hazardous Materials:

    The rule also addresses the handling and storage of hazardous materials, including the prescription of maximum allowable quantities and the increasing of regulatory fees.

    LESSON 3. FIRE SAFETY MEASURES, EMERGENCY, EVACUATION PLAN

    Fire safety measures involve both prevention and response to reduce the risk of fire and ensure a safe evacuation. Key actions include installing smoke alarms, developing escape plans, and practicing fire drills. Additional measures include proper electrical maintenance, safe storage of flammable materials, and regular inspections of heating equipment.

    FIRE SAFETY CHECKLIST FOR THE WORKPLACE

    • Does your property have clear and accessible escape routes?

    • Is fire fighting equipment available throughout the entire property?

    • Do you have a fully working, regularly tested fire alarm?

    • Do you have a set of fire precautions advertised for your employees?

    • Are your staff regularly trained in fire safety matters?

    • Are all of your staff taken into consideration in your evacuation plans?

    • Do you have a sectioned area for smokers on your premises?

    • Are your fire extinguishers serviced and replaced on a regular basis?

    • Are all of your electrical systems and appliances maintained and checked regularly?
